About OCR GCSE Latin (J282)

OCR GCSE Latin is a two-component course assessed entirely by examination. Both papers are worth 50 marks each and together form 100% of the final grade.

  • Component 01 — Language (50 marks, 1h 30m): comprehension questions and translation of unseen Latin passages drawn from the defined vocabulary list. You must recognise any inflected form of any word on the list, parse verbs and nouns correctly, and handle all the syntax constructions on the specification.
  • Component 02 — Literature (50 marks, 1h 30m): two prescribed literary texts (one prose, one verse). Questions test comprehension, literary analysis, and comparison of style. Set texts change each cycle — check the OCR website for the current prescription.

The defined vocabulary list contains approximately 430 words. Every word on it is included in this reference, along with principal parts for verbs and genitive forms for nouns, so you can generate any inflected form.

How Latin works — one paragraph: Latin is an inflected language: word endings change to show grammatical role, so word order is flexible. The six cases tell you what each noun does (subject, object, possession, etc.); verb endings encode person, number, tense, voice, and mood all at once. The key to reading Latin is identifying the finite verb first (it anchors the clause), then finding its subject (nominative), then the object (accusative), then working out the modifiers. Most GCSE passages use straightforward sentence structures — the difficulty lies in recognising inflected forms quickly. This tool is designed to help you do exactly that.

Nouns in Latin are declined: their endings change to show their grammatical role (case). Every noun belongs to one of five declensions, identified by the genitive singular ending. In OCR Component 01, you must recognise any case in any declension — the genitive singular (dictionary form) is your key to identifying the stem. Nouns appear in all six cases; the most tested are nominative (subject), accusative (object), genitive (possession), and ablative (with/by/from).

Adjectives

Adjectives agree with their noun in gender, number, and case. The most common pattern is 2-1-2 (like bonus), where masculine and neuter follow 2nd declension and feminine follows 1st. Third-declension adjectives have one or two terminations and follow 3rd declension patterns. In OCR papers, adjectives frequently appear as predicates (rex erat magnus) and as attributive modifiers — always agree them with their noun before translating.

Comparison of Adjectives

Regular comparatives add -ior (m/f) / -ius (n) to the stem; superlatives add -issimus, -a, -um (or -errimus for stems ending in -er). Comparison uses quam + same case, or the ablative of comparison. Superlative commonly takes a partitive genitive: fortissimus omnium — bravest of all.

Verbs

Latin verbs are conjugated for person, number, tense, voice (active/passive), and mood (indicative/subjunctive/imperative). Four conjugations are identified by their infinitive: 1st -āre, 2nd -ēre, 3rd -ĕre, 4th -īre. In OCR Component 01, every tense of the active indicative may appear, as may the present and imperfect passive, the perfect passive, and the imperfect subjunctive. Click any verb in the vocabulary to see its full paradigm.

Four conjugations, identified by infinitive ending: 1st -āre, 2nd -ēre, 3rd -ĕre, 4th -īre. In the tables below, stem and ending are colour-coded.

Voice: Active and Passive

Active voice: the subject performs the action — miles hostem necat (the soldier kills the enemy). Active endings: -o/-m, -s, -t, -mus, -tis, -nt.

Passive voice: the subject receives the action — hostis a milite necatur (the enemy is killed by the soldier). Passive endings: -or/-r, -ris, -tur, -mur, -mini, -ntur. The agent (who performs the action) is expressed by a/ab + ablative. The instrument (what does the action) uses the ablative alone.

The perfect passive is formed differently: perfect passive participle + esse (agreeing with the subject in gender and number): amatus est (he was loved), amata est (she was loved), amati sunt (they were loved).

Mood: Indicative, Subjunctive, Imperative

Indicative: states facts — "the soldier fights", "the queen was wise". This is the default mood for main verbs in statements and questions.

Subjunctive: expresses possibility, desire, or doubt, and is required in most subordinate clauses (purpose, result, fear, indirect command, cum narrative, indirect question). It is never the main verb of a simple factual statement. Key recognition tip: the present subjunctive of 1st conjugation uses -e- (amem, ames, amet…); for 2nd/3rd/4th it uses -a- (moneam, regam, audiam…). The imperfect subjunctive is always the present infinitive + personal endings (amarem, monerem, regerem…).

Imperative: direct commands. Singular = 2nd pp. stem for most verbs (ama! mone! rege! audi!). Negative commands use noli/nolite + infinitive.

Perfect Active Indicative

The perfect describes a single completed action in the past: venit = he came (and arrived). Translates as "did —" or "has —ed". Form: perfect stem (3rd principal part minus -i) + the endings below. The perfect stem is often unpredictable — learn all four principal parts. Common patterns: -avi/-evi/-ivi (sigmatic: amavi, monui, audivi); reduplication (cucurri from curro); lengthened vowel (veni from venio); -si (dixi from dico).

Pluperfect Active

The pluperfect expresses an action completed before another past event: "had done". Signal: perfect stem + -era- + personal endings. milites qui urbem ceperant discesserant — the soldiers who had captured the city had departed. Think of it as the "past of the past". In English narrative it's often the first event mentioned last: "the queen spoke — her son had already left".

Forms: amaveram (I had loved), amaveras, amaverat, amaveramus, amaveratis, amaverant.

Perfect & Pluperfect Passive

Formed with the perfect passive participle (4th principal part, e.g. amatus/amata/amatum) + the appropriate form of esse. The participle must agree with the subject in gender, number, and case.

Perfect passive: PPP + sum/es/est/sumus/estis/sunt — amatus est = he was loved; amata est = she was loved; amati sunt = they (m.) were loved.

Pluperfect passive: PPP + eram/eras/erat… — amatus erat = he had been loved.

The agent is expressed with a/ab + ablative: a regina amatus est = he was loved by the queen.

Subjunctive

The subjunctive is the "mood of subordination" — it never stands alone as a main verb in a simple factual statement. It appears in purpose clauses, result clauses, fear clauses, indirect commands, indirect questions, and cum narrative clauses. In an exam, seeing a subjunctive is a signal to ask: what type of clause is this?

Recognising subjunctive forms — the characteristic vowel shifts: present subjunctive of 1st conj. uses -e- (amem, ames, amet…); 2nd/3rd/4th use -a- (moneam, regam, audiam…). Mnemonic: "she may eat a little bit" → 1st uses e, rest use a. The imperfect subjunctive is always present infinitive + personal endings: amarem, amares, amaret… — if you can spot the infinitive inside the form, it's imperfect subjunctive.

Tense in subjunctive follows the sequence of tenses: a primary main verb (present/future/perfect with "have") takes present or perfect subjunctive in its clause; a historic main verb (imperfect/perfect narrative/pluperfect) takes imperfect or pluperfect subjunctive. The present subjunctive indicates the same or future time; the perfect, prior time.

Participles

Participles are verbal adjectives — they must agree with a noun in gender, number and case, while also carrying the verbal idea. The ablative absolute (noun + participle, both in the ablative) is one of the most frequently tested constructions in OCR papers. Always check whether the participle is present (simultaneous action) or perfect passive (completed, passive action).

Deponent & Semi-deponent Verbs

Deponent verbs have passive forms but active meaning. All their participles are active in meaning. They are conjugated exactly like the passive of their conjugation class. Semi-deponent verbs are active in the present system but deponent (passive forms, active meaning) in the perfect system:

VerbMeaningPerfect (deponent form)
audeo, auderedareausus sum
gaudeo, gaudererejoice, be gladgavisus sum
soleo, solerebe accustomed tosolitus sum
fido, fideretrustfisus sum
Semi-deponents are easy to misread: ausus sum looks passive ("I was dared") but means "I dared." Always check whether the verb is listed as semi-deponent in the dictionary.

Adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs and are indeclinable — they never change form. Many are formed regularly from adjectives; others are independent words. Key Syntax

Subordinate Clauses with the Subjunctive

Purpose Clauses (Final Clauses)

Express the aim or intention of the main action. Use ut (so that, in order to) or ne (so that … not, in order not to) + subjunctive. Tense follows the sequence of tenses: present subjunctive after a main-tense verb; imperfect subjunctive after a historic-tense verb.

milites venerunt ut urbem defenderent — the soldiers came in order to defend the city.

fugiunt ne capiantur — they flee so that they are not captured.

Key signal words: ut, ne, qui/quae/quod (relative purpose), ad + gerundive.

Result Clauses (Consecutive Clauses)

Express what actually happens as a consequence. Main clause typically contains a "pointer" word: tam (so), tantus (so great), tot (so many), talis (such), ita/sic (thus). Then ut (that) or ut non (that … not) + subjunctive.

tam fortis erat ut hostes fugerent — he was so brave that the enemies fled.

tantus erat clamor ut nemo audiret — the noise was so great that no one heard.

Distinguish from purpose: result clauses follow a pointer word and describe what happened, not what was intended.

Fear Clauses

After verbs of fearing (timeo, metuo, vereor): use ne + subjunctive to express what you fear WILL happen; use ut or ne non + subjunctive to express what you fear will NOT happen. This is the opposite of purpose — ne = "that", ut = "that … not".

timeo ne hostes veniant — I fear that the enemies are coming (will come).

timeo ut dux veniat — I fear that the general will not come.

Memory aid: after fear verbs, ne = "that" and ut = "that not" — the exact reverse of purpose.

Indirect Commands

After verbs of ordering, begging, persuading, advising (impero, rogo, oro, moneo, hortor, persuadeo, peto): use ut (to, that) or ne (not to) + subjunctive. Not infinitive — this is the key difference from indirect statement.

imperavit militibus ut murum aedificarent — he ordered the soldiers to build the wall.

rogavit nos ne discederemus — he asked us not to leave.

With impero + dative; with rogo, oro + accusative.

Cum Clauses

cum + indicative = "when" (at the exact time that — temporal, factual).

cum + imperfect subjunctive = "when / while" (circumstantial — describes setting).

cum + pluperfect subjunctive = "when / after" (circumstantial — completed action).

cum + imperfect subjunctive can also be causal ("since") or concessive ("although") — context decides.

cum servi laborarent, dominus dormiebat — while the slaves were working, the master was sleeping.

cum hostes vicisset, dux laetus erat — when/after he had defeated the enemies, the general was happy.

cum rex abessent, cives laeti erant — although the king was away, the citizens were happy (concessive).

Indirect Questions

After verbs of asking, knowing, not knowing, telling (rogo, quaero, scio, nescio, dico): the question word (quis, ubi, cur, quomodo, num, utrum…an) + subjunctive. Tense follows sequence.

rogavit cur miles fugisset — he asked why the soldier had fled.

nescio quis hoc fecerit — I do not know who did this.

Use num (whether) for yes/no questions; utrum … an for either/or; direct questions use indicative.

Participles

Participles are verbal adjectives — they agree with a noun in case, number, and gender.

Present Active Participle

Formed from the present stem + -ans/-antis (1st/2nd conj) or -ens/-entis (3rd/4th conj). Means "—ing" or "while —ing".

miles currens cecidit — the soldier, while running, fell.

ancilla cibum portans intravit — the slave-girl carrying the food entered.

Perfect Passive Participle (PPP)

The 4th principal part, declining like bonus, -a, -um. Means "having been —ed". Often combined with esse to form perfect passive tenses.

urbs capta erat — the city had been captured.

milites vulnerati fugerunt — the wounded soldiers fled (lit. the soldiers, having been wounded, fled).

Future Active Participle

PPP stem + -urus, -ura, -urum. Means "about to —" or "intending to —". Used with esse in indirect statement to indicate future time relative to the main verb.

dicit milites victuros esse — he says that the soldiers will conquer.

nuntius urbem capturus erat — the messenger was about to capture the city.

Ablative Absolute

A noun/pronoun + participle in the ablative — forming a self-contained phrase describing the circumstances of the main action. Translates as "when/since/although/after + noun + participle".

hostibus victis, dux domum rediit — after the enemies had been defeated, the general returned home.

rege absente, senatores conjuraverunt — since/while the king was absent, the senators conspired.

The noun and participle in the ablative absolute cannot be the subject of the main verb. If no participle, use the noun + noun/adj construction: Caesare duce — with Caesar as leader.

Relative Clauses

Introduced by the relative pronoun qui, quae, quod (who, which, that). The pronoun agrees with its antecedent (the noun it refers back to) in gender and number, but its case is determined by its function within the relative clause.

Conditional Clauses

Open conditions (likely/possible): si + indicative (any tense) → indicative. Translated as "if … then".

si hoc facis, errras — if you are doing this, you are wrong.

Unfulfilled/remote conditions: si + imperfect subjunctive in both clauses = present unfulfilled ("if … were … would be"); si + pluperfect subjunctive = past unfulfilled ("if … had … would have").

si rex adessent, milites pugnarent — if the king were here, the soldiers would fight.

si rex adfuissent, milites pugnavissent — if the king had been here, the soldiers would have fought.

nisi = "if … not / unless".

Indirect Statement (Accusative + Infinitive)

After verbs of saying, thinking, knowing, believing (dico, puto, credo, scio, audio, video): the subject of the reported clause → accusative; the verb → infinitive.

dicit reginam esse bonam — he says that the queen is good.

credebant milites vicisse — they believed that the soldiers had conquered.

sperant nos venturos esse — they hope that we will come.

Tense of infinitive is relative to the main verb: present infinitive = same time; perfect infinitive = before; future infinitive = after.

Reflexive pronoun se in indirect statement refers back to the subject of the main verb: dixit se venturum esse — he said that he (himself) would come.

Gerunds and Gerundives

Gerund (verbal noun, always active, always singular): gen -andi/-endi/-endi/-iendi, dat/abl -ando/-endo, acc -andum/-endum.

amor legendi — a love of reading; ad legendum — for the purpose of reading.

Gerundive (verbal adjective, passive, "ought to be / must be"): declines like bonus, -a, -um. Often replaces the gerund when an object is present.

epistula amanda est — the letter must be loved. Agent uses dative: mihi epistula amanda est — I must love the letter.

Gerundive of purpose: ad + gerundive agreeing with a noun = "for the purpose of —ing the noun". ad pacem petendam — for the purpose of seeking peace.

Comparison of Adjectives

Comparative: stem + -ior (m/f), -ius (n). Declines as a 3rd-declension adjective. Means "more —" or "rather —".

Superlative: stem + -issimus, -issima, -issimum (or -errimus for adjectives ending in -er). Declines like bonus. Means "most —" or "very —".

Comparison uses quam (than) with same case, or ablative of comparison without quam.

hic miles fortior quam ille est — this soldier is braver than that one.

fortissimus omnium erat — he was the bravest of all.

Irregular: bonus → melior → optimus; malus → peior → pessimus; magnus → maior → maximus; parvus → minor → minimus; multus → plus → plurimus.

Uses of the Infinitive

Complementary infinitive: after verbs like possum, volo, nolo, malo, debeo, soleo, coepi, constituo. vult pugnare — he wants to fight.

Historic infinitive: present infinitive used instead of imperfect in vivid narrative. cives fugere, hostes sequi — the citizens were fleeing, the enemies were pursuing.

Indirect statement: see above (acc + inf construction).
